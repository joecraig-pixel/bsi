Conveyor System
  • 1

    Monorail Conveyor

    Overhead I-beam track with trolleys & hooks. Parts hang and travel through the blast zone. Driven by a variable speed drive for adjustable exposure time.

    Fully enclosed steel cabinet with manganese liner plates to protect structural steel. Access doors for maintenance and blast pattern checking.

  • 3

    Shot Reclaim System

    Floor screw conveyor + bucket elevator returns spent abrasive from the hopper pit back to the separator for cleaning and re-use.

Processing Systems

    4 × Blast Wheel Units

    25BD250 Wheelabrator BD wheels positioned to provide 360° coverage. Wheel rotation directions to be confirmed at inspection.

  • 5

    LP Magnetic Separator

    Two-stage separator removes sand, fines, and metallic waste from recirculating abrasive before returning to overhead storage hopper.

  • 6

    Dust Collector

    Negative pressure system captures fine particles from the separator air wash and blast cabinet. Filters must be maintained for peak separator performance.

How to interpret amp efficiency

Formula: % Efficiency = ((Actual Amps − NLC Amps) ÷ Max Blast Amps) × 100  |  Lbs/min = 25 × (Actual − NLC)  |  Max = 25 × (FLA − NLC) = 650 lbs/min per wheel. Target ≥ 90% overall for optimal cleaning results.

Hot Spot Definition

The hot spot contains ~70% of blast intensity. A misaligned hot spot dramatically increases wear on cabinet liners and reduces cleaning efficiency. Always verify after any wheel component change.

Three Verification Methods:

  1. Hot Spot Test Plate — 12-ga steel plate placed in blast zone, run 45 sec, LOTO and inspect burnished impact pattern
  2. Infrared Method — IR camera scan of cabinet liners post-blast; hot zone reveals intensity concentration without opening machine
  3. Paint Method — Coat target plate with light contrasting paint; blast removes paint at impact zone, unmistakably showing hot spot location and shape
Blast Pattern Check – Step by Step
  • 1

    LOTO – Lock out / Tag out

    Turn main disconnect OFF. Apply all locks. Follow OSHA 29 CFR 1910.147.

    DANGER
  • 2

    Install Target Plate

    Secure a 12-gauge, 12″×48″ steel target plate in the blast zone at 90° to the wheel and perpendicular to blast flow.

  • 3

    Start System (without conveyor)

    Start blast wheels, separator, dust collector. Do NOT run monorail conveyor during test.

    CAUTION
  • 4

    Blast for 45 Seconds

    Use cycle timer or hold pattern test pushbutton for exactly 45 seconds per wheel.

  • 5

    Allow Abrasive to Clear

    Wait 30+ seconds for dust and abrasive to clear before opening the cabinet.

  • 6

    LOTO & Inspect Plate

    Lock out again. Inspect target plate. Hot spot should be centered on work piece path. Adjust cage ≤ 3/8″–3/4″ per notch.

⛔

Control Cage Caution – Fragile Cast Components

Blast wheel wear parts are high-hardness cast alloy. Use MODERATE force when tapping the control cage to rotate it. Do NOT use cast wear parts that show chips, cracks, or broken sections — they will disintegrate at speed and cause serious injury or equipment damage.

Magnetic Drum – Release Point Setting Reference
Release Point Setting — Key Points
  • Release Point — where drum shell rotates away from the magnetic field. Good, magnetically-responsive abrasive releases here and falls into the clean hopper.
  • Sand / Fines — non-magnetic, drop off the drum before the release point into the sand discharge chute.
  • Magnet Zone — stationary inside the rotating shell. Rotate the core position to move the release point and tune separation quality.
  • Burden Depth — abrasive layer thickness across the drum face, controlled by the sliding baffle. Too thick = poor separation. Too thin = low throughput.
  • Verification — if usable S390 appears in the sand discharge sample, the release point is too early. Rotate the magnet core to move the release point further around the drum.
Primary LP Separator – Maintenance Items
  • A

    Upper Screw & Rotary Screen

    Inspect screen for tears or blockage. Rotate freely? Check screw flights for wear.

  • B

    Sliding Baffle

    Adjust baffle to control abrasive curtain depth across the magnetic drum face. Creates effective "burden depth."

  • C

    Burden Plate

    Spreads abrasive evenly across drum width. Inspect for wear and proper positioning.

  • D

    Magnetic Drum System

    Stationary magnetic core inside rotating drum shell. Verify magnet strength annually with gauss meter.

  • E

    Air Wash Separator   Current Airflow Reading:

    Adjustable airflow removes fine particles & sand. Take timed 1-minute samples from sand discharge to measure waste rate.

  • F

    Abrasive Screen Analysis

    Perform sieve analysis monthly on separator sample to verify operating mix versus new abrasive benchmark.

Secondary LP Separator – Maintenance Items
  • A

    Feed Pipe/Hose & Inlet Pipe

    Inspect for abrasive wear-through. Critical – a failed feed pipe contaminates cleaned abrasive.

  • B

    Inlet Chamber Baffles

    Distribute abrasive evenly across drum. Misaligned baffles create uneven separation efficiency.

  • C

    Magnetic Drum System

    Same as primary – check magnet core and drum shell clearance. Confirm proper rotation speed.

  • D

    Adjustable Angle & Lower Hoppers

    Adjust discharge angle to direct separated metallic fines to scrap container, not back into abrasive flow.

  • E

    Sand/Metallic Fines Screen Analysis

    Screen all discharge streams for good reusable abrasive being lost as waste. Track in oz/min.

  • F

    Ventilation Ducts

    Check for plugging – blocked ducts prevent proper air wash function and separator performance degrades immediately.

The Four (4) Abrasive Consumption Factors
  • 1

    Wheel HP / Wheel Flow

    More HP = more pounds of abrasive thrown per minute. 25 lbs per working amp above no-load. This machine max = 4 × 650 lbs/min.

  • 2

    Velocity of Abrasive

    Determined by wheel diameter and RPM using the formula: Dia. × RPM ÷ 180. For this machine: 25 × 1,800 ÷ 180 = 250 FPM abrasive velocity. Changing RPM shifts blast pattern and energy delivery.

  • 3

    Size of Abrasive

    Larger shot = more kinetic energy per impact, fewer impacts per pound. S390 is the foundry balance point for iron castings.

  • 4

    Hardness of Abrasive

    Harder shot lasts longer and delivers more impacts per pound before breakdown. Soft or worn abrasive produces fines that contaminate the mix.

When the Operating Mix Goes Wrong
⛔

Hot spot shifts unexpectedly

A change in average particle size (too many fines) shifts and lengthens the blast pattern. Check sieve analysis immediately.

⚠

Finish quality changes

Profile (anchor pattern) depth changes when mix ratio drifts. Monitor with profile gauge – target reading on this machine from data.

⚠

Line speed must decrease to get results

Degraded mix needs more exposure time. This is a hidden cost that signals process control failure before amp efficiency shows it.

Abrasive Addition Rule

Make small, consistent additions at the natural breakdown rate. For this machine, track additions vs. blast hours and consumption trends. A properly managed mix at full amp efficiency delivers maximum kinetic cleaning energy with minimum abrasive waste.

BSI Key Principle

Abrasive consumption is driven by 4 things: HP/wheel flow, velocity, size, hardness — NOT leaks. Leaks are abrasive waste. Track them separately and attack each discharge point with timed 1-minute samples.

What to Measure — All 4 Discharge Points
  • 1

    Mag Separator Sand Discharge

    Primary separator sand & fines discharge — should be sand/dust only, no usable shot. Take 1-min timed sample, screen to #25, weigh what's retained.

  • 2

    Metallic Fines Discharge

    Secondary separator metallic discharge. Should be broken shot only. If good usable S-390 is in here, adjust separator airflow and baffle position.

  • 3

    Dust Collector Carry-Out

    Dust collector outlet — screen the catch. Any material retained on #80 screen is usable abrasive being wasted. This is your airflow calibration indicator.

  • 4

    Cabinet Leaks (Estimated)

    Finger seals, roof slots, door gaps — what you can't directly measure. Calculated as the gap between purchased tons and all measured losses. Fix the biggest leaks first for highest ROI.

Notes on Abrasive Waste Control

Controlling this cost starts with identifying problem areas — and this can only be done with timed samples from all discharge points including the dust collector for good usable abrasive.

ℹ

Finger Seals & Roof Slot Seal Improvements

The fastest payback improvements on this machine are: (1) roof slot finger seal replacement, (2) monorail entry/exit curtain upgrade, (3) door gasket inspection. Each oz/min saved = ~3.4 tons/year at this machine's run schedule.

ℹ

Abrasive Adder & Dynamic Baffle ROI

With better process controls and equipment upgrades including an Abrasive Adder & Dynamic Baffle, potential abrasive consumption savings = 10 tons/yr. Value calculation: Benefit (savings) minus Cost ($10,000 investment).

Module 11 · Diagnostics
Troubleshooting Guide
Common problems, root causes, and corrective actions – click any symptom to expand
⚡ High amp draw – wheels over-amping at startup ▼
Cause: Abrasive overload, clogged feed spout, or impeller misalignment
  • Check abrasive hopper gate – may be fully open instead of metered
  • Inspect feed spout for blockage or buildup
  • Verify impeller is correctly seated and centered
  • Check control cage opening alignment – misaligned cage dumps extra abrasive on blades
  • Compare NLC amps with last known value – increase indicates bearing wear
📊 Low wheel amp efficiency – wheels running light (< 70%) ▼
Cause: Low abrasive level, worn impeller, blocked feed line, or impeller timing off
  • Fill abrasive hopper to full – low hopper = low wheel flow
  • Inspect impeller for wear on vane leading edges
  • Check feed gate is fully open during operation
  • Inspect bucket elevator for slippage – verify delivery rate to hopper
  • Review separator – may be over-rejecting good abrasive
🎯 Blast pattern shifted – parts not cleaning properly ▼
Cause: Control cage rotated, worn components, abrasive mix change, or RPM change
  • Perform blast pattern target plate test immediately
  • Check control cage position indicator – compare with last recorded setting
  • Inspect blades for uneven wear pattern – asymmetric wear shifts hot spot
  • Perform sieve analysis – an increase in fines percentage shifts the pattern
  • Verify wheel RPM matches original design spec
  • Adjust control cage in 3/8″–3/4″ notch increments only
💥 Abnormal vibration or banging noise from wheel housing ▼
Cause: Broken or missing blade, loose blade, worn bearing, or foreign object
  • STOP MACHINE IMMEDIATELY – LOTO before any investigation
  • Open wheel housing and inspect all 8 blades for cracks, chips, or missing sections
  • Do NOT operate with damaged blades – they will fragment at high speed
  • Check blade retention hardware (centering plate and bare wheel)
  • Inspect bare wheel for cracks – replace if any damage found
  • Inspect motor bearing condition (heat, noise when motor spun by hand after isolation)
⚙ Separator not removing sand – sand in blasting mix ▼
Cause: Insufficient airflow, plugged ventilation duct, misadjusted baffle, or high burden depth
  • Check dust collector filter differential pressure – clogged filters reduce airflow
  • Inspect all ventilation ductwork for sand/abrasive plugging
  • Adjust separator air wash damper to increase suction
  • Reduce sliding baffle opening to thin abrasive curtain across magnetic drum
  • Perform separator sample timed test – screen and measure sand content
  • Take timed samples from all discharge points to isolate the problem area
🔌 Wheel motor trips on overload – thermal cutout ▼
Cause: Abrasive overload, damaged bearing, single-phasing, or incorrect overload setting
  • Check all 3 phases at motor terminals – verify balanced voltage (within 1%)
  • Verify overload relay is set to motor nameplate FLA (not wheel max blast amps)
  • Inspect motor bearings for overheating or noise
  • Reduce abrasive feed rate temporarily and observe amp draw
  • Check feed gate is not stuck open beyond normal metered flow
  • Contact BSI technical support if issue persists after basic checks
🌡 Infrared camera – blast pattern hot spot analysis & uneven wear diagnosis ▼
Application: Use an IR thermal camera to visualize the blast pattern hot spot location and identify uneven wear distribution inside the cabinet — without opening it.
  • Hot Spot Pattern Check: After a short blast run, scan the cabinet liner panels and work zone with the IR camera through an inspection port or immediately after opening. Warmer zones (brighter on thermal image) reveal exactly where 70% of blast intensity is concentrated — confirm the hot spot is centered on the work path.
  • Liner Wear Mapping: Thinner liner areas radiate heat faster and appear hotter on IR after a blast cycle. Use this to prioritize liner replacement before a catastrophic breakthrough — especially on the curved top and side liners.
  • Wheel Housing Hot Spots: A wheel bearing or housing running hot will show on IR before it fails. Scan all 4 wheel housings after a full production run; any housing noticeably hotter than the others warrants bearing inspection.
  • IR cameras do not require LOTO for external scans — but always LOTO before entering the blast zone or touching any component.
  • Document IR images with date and wheel number. Trending thermal images over time is one of the most powerful predictive maintenance tools available for blast equipment.
⚡ Infrared camera – LOTO verification & hot wire tryout (electrical safety) ▼
Application: Use IR thermal imaging as a secondary verification tool during LOTO procedures to confirm de-energization and detect unexpected live conductors, hot connections, or failed isolation points before personnel enter the machine.
  • LOTO Tryout – IR Verification: After applying all locks and performing the standard LOTO tryout (attempt-to-start), scan the main disconnect, motor starters, and control panel with the IR camera. Any energized conductor or component that was not properly isolated will appear significantly hotter than de-energized components.
  • Hot Wire Detection: IR cameras can identify "hot wires" — conductors carrying current that were missed during isolation — even through panel covers. A live 460V conductor running inside a conduit will show a heat signature detectable at the panel entry point.
  • Loose or Corroded Connections: Resistance at a loose lug or corroded terminal creates heat. Scan all motor terminal boxes and starter connections quarterly; any connection running more than 10°C above ambient is a failure risk that must be corrected before it becomes a fault.
  • IR scanning is a supplement to — never a replacement for — proper LOTO procedures per OSHA 29 CFR 1910.147. Always perform the full LOTO sequence first.
  • Use a FLIR or equivalent IR camera rated for electrical inspection (minimum 320×240 resolution). Capture and save images before and after any corrective action for the maintenance record.
  • Any "hot wire" finding must be reported to the plant electrician immediately. Do not re-energize until the source of unexpected heat is identified and corrected.
🌡 Infrared camera – hopper & feed pipe obstruction limiting abrasive flow and full load amps ▼
Application: Use IR thermal imaging to locate bridging, blockages, or partial obstructions in the overhead abrasive storage hopper, feed gates, and feed pipes that starve wheels of abrasive — preventing them from reaching full load amps and rated efficiency.
  • Hopper Bridging Detection: A hopper that has bridged (abrasive arched across the outlet) will show a cold void zone on IR directly above the bridge point while abrasive piled above the bridge retains heat from the building environment. Compare thermal images of a full flowing hopper versus a suspect one — a hard horizontal cold line across the hopper wall indicates a bridge.
  • Feed Gate Restriction: Scan the feed gate housing during operation. A gate that is not opening fully due to wear, debris, or mechanical failure will restrict flow — the downstream feed pipe will run cooler than normal (less abrasive mass = less thermal mass). A properly flowing feed pipe will show a consistent warm signature from the moving abrasive stream.
  • Feed Pipe Blockage: A partially or fully blocked feed pipe will show a sharp cold zone at the blockage point and a warm buildup of stationary abrasive just upstream of it. Scan each wheel's feed pipe from the hopper outlet down to the feed spout entry during a blast cycle — any cold-dead section warrants immediate investigation.
  • Amp Correlation: If a wheel is running low actual amps despite the global hopper being full, IR scan that wheel's feed system first before adjusting the separator or adding abrasive. A starved wheel from a blocked feed pipe is the most common cause of a single wheel under-amping while the others run normal.
  • After clearing any obstruction, re-read the wheel amps within 2–3 minutes of restart. Full load amps should climb to the target range as the feed pipe re-primes with abrasive flow. If amps do not recover, the obstruction may be partial or located further upstream at the gate or hopper outlet.
  • Document IR images of all four feed pipe runs at normal operation as a baseline. This makes future comparisons fast and removes guesswork during a troubleshooting call.
🌡 Sand or shot buildup in ductwork – IR detection & remediation ▼
Cause: Progressive buildup of sand, fines, or shot in separator and dust collector ductwork reduces airflow, degrades separator performance, and can cause structural duct failure or fire risk from friction heat.
  • IR Detection Method: Scan all ductwork runs — especially elbows, transitions, and drops — with the IR camera during or immediately after machine operation. Sections with heavy buildup insulate the duct wall, causing them to appear cooler (less heat transfer from process air) or show a distinct cold mass signature at the bottom of horizontal runs.
  • Elbow Wear-Through Risk: Elbows carrying abrasive-laden air erode from the outside radius inward. An elbow near wear-through will show a hot spot on IR as thin metal transfers heat rapidly. Mark, schedule replacement, and do not operate until repaired — a blowout sprays abrasive into the building at high velocity.
  • Check separator inlet duct, air wash duct, and dust collector inlet duct quarterly. Vertical runs are especially prone to sand bridging after separator upsets.
  • Tap the duct with a rubber mallet while scanning — a buildup section will sound and feel dead/solid vs. hollow. The IR image will confirm what the tap test suggests.
  • Remediation: LOTO the machine and separator fan. Open the clean-out ports (or fabricate access plates if none exist). Rod out or vacuum the buildup. Never use compressed air to blow out a plugged duct — it redistributes fine silica dust as a respiratory hazard.
  • After cleaning, verify separator air wash airflow has returned to normal by taking a timed abrasive discharge sample and comparing to the pre-plug baseline. Airflow restoration is confirmed when sand discharge rate returns to the established target.
